Why Professional Biohazard Remediation Is Different From Cleaning<h2>
The core difference is not equipment, though our equipment is specialized. The core difference is the regulatory and scientific framework that governs how the work is done.
OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard requires that any work involving potential exposure to blood or other potentially infectious materials (OPIM) operate under universal precautions: treating all biological material as potentially infectious regardless of what is known about the individuals involved. This is not an abundance of caution. It is the legally mandated approach based on the fact that the hepatitis B virus can survive on a dry environmental surface for up to two weeks, and hepatitis C can survive similarly. Dried blood that looks desiccated is still within its pathogen survival window.
Standard household cleaning products, including bleach at typical household dilution, do not meet the tuberculocidal spectrum that EPA-registered biohazard disinfectants must demonstrate. Meeting that spectrum means documented kill claims against hepatitis B, hepatitis C, MRSA, and tuberculosis as the benchmark pathogens for surface decontamination. When Comer technicians apply disinfectant to a surface, that product's kill claims have been verified by the EPA before it reached our equipment.

What Complete Remediation Looks Like<h2>
A complete biohazard remediation is not finished when the visible material has been removed. It is finished when the space has been decontaminated, the decontamination has been verified, and the regulated waste has left the property in the appropriate containers.
The sequence follows a containment-to-clearance progression. The affected area is established as a hot zone before any work begins, with physical barriers and appropriate signage separating the contaminated space from the rest of the property. All work inside the hot zone is performed in full PPE: Tyvek coveralls with taped seams, double nitrile gloves, full-face respirator or N95 with eye protection. Contaminated materials, including all porous materials that have absorbed biological fluid, are placed in sealed biohazard containers and removed from the property. The remaining surfaces are cleaned and treated with EPA-registered disinfectant at the appropriate concentration and dwell time. ATP testing at the conclusion confirms that the surface has no detectable biological residue. A certificate of decontamination documents the completed scope.
