What OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard Requires in #State<h2>
OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) establishes the minimum required safety protocol for any work involving potential exposure to blood or other potentially infectious materials. In #State, as in every state, this standard applies to biohazard remediation work.
The standard's universal precautions doctrine requires treating all blood and bodily fluid as potentially infectious regardless of the source or the circumstances. The reason for this specific requirement is that the pathogen status of the individuals involved in any scene cannot be determined by visual inspection, and several pathogens of concern, most significantly the hepatitis B virus, remain viable on dry environmental surfaces for up to two weeks. A scene that is two days old and involves dried material is not a safer scene from a pathogen standpoint. The same protective protocols apply.
For #State families and property owners, this standard is the reason a professional biohazard company does things that seem more extensive than what common sense might suggest. It is not overcaution. It is the legally mandated approach based on what the science of pathogen survival requires.

Hidden Contamination in #State Properties<h2>
One of the most important pieces of information for any #State property owner dealing with a biohazard scene is that the extent of contamination is almost always larger than the visible area. This is a physical property of biological fluids, not a judgment about the severity of the situation.
Blood and other biological fluids are liquid. They follow gravity and capillary action through every porous material they contact. On a carpeted floor, blood at the surface travels through the carpet pile, into the backing, through the pad, and potentially into the subfloor. The stain visible at the carpet surface represents the entry point of the contamination, not its full extent. Lifting the carpet at a #State scene routinely reveals that the staining area below is larger than what was visible above.
Structural penetration matters because pathogens in the material below the visible surface are not addressed by surface cleaning alone. Professional remediation of a blood or biohazard scene in a #State property must assess below the visible surface and address whatever depth the material has reached.

Unattended Death Cleanup Across #State<h2>
In #State, unattended death scenes present a range of conditions depending on how much time elapsed before discovery. A discovery made within 24 to 48 hours involves biological material that is primarily still in its liquid or semi-liquid state and has not yet penetrated deeply into structural elements. A discovery made after days or weeks involves decomposition byproducts including biological gases and fluid that have penetrated flooring, may have reached the subfloor and lower wall assemblies, and have produced odor compounds that have bound to structural surfaces at the molecular level.
The volatile amines of decomposition, including putrescine and cadaverine, penetrate porous materials and cannot be removed by odor masking or standard cleaning. They require enzymatic treatment that breaks down the organic molecular compound and hydroxyl generator technology that neutralizes airborne compounds. Standard air fresheners or even industrial deodorizers applied over these compounds mask rather than eliminate. The odor returns as the masking agent dissipates.
